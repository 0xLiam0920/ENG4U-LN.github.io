① Three connections (form ↔ history)

Survivance, the sonnet. The octave is Durham's 1839 verdict and the way the Act of Union erased French from the legislature. The volta at line 9 ("But banished is not buried") is where survivance kicks in. Those sealed fourteen lines are a culture holding its shape under pressure.
Two Solitudes, the contrapuntal. The left column is English duty, the right is French refusal (1917 conscription and Regulation 17). Read straight across and both columns end up in the same grave at Flanders. The form makes the reader act out the division.
October, and After, free verse on purpose. The broken syntax matches how fast October 1970 moved, then the lines stretch out for the referendum years. Bullets become ballots.

② Anticipated questions

Through-line? It's one argument that never gets settled, just carried in two languages. The substance stays the same; the method keeps changing. Watch the repeating images: the tongue, the seed under snow, the river, the house. The seed in Poem 1 comes back in Poem 3, transformed rather than resolved.
Differing perspectives? Every poem gives both sides and then the counter-argument. The Quebec Act was accommodation, not only oppression. The Van Doos served, and plenty of anglophone farmers also hated conscription. The FLQ's violence isn't the same thing as Quebec's real grievance (the War Measures Act detained roughly 500 people with no charge).
One device, in depth? Paradox. "A liberty suspended to save the liberty" packs the whole War Measures Act debate into five words.
What would you change? The across-reading in the contrapuntal reads more like a list than a lyric. I gave up some smoothness to get the structural payoff, so it's a trade-off I made on purpose, not a weakness.
Present-day link? The 1995 result (50.58% No) and the way Bill 101 still shapes daily life show the "two tongues" question is alive, not just history.
